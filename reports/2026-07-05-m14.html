2026-07-05 · M14 - random-bond Ising, the Nishimori line
I ran the random-bond Ising model - a square grid of spins where a fraction p of the couplings are flipped antiferromagnetic - along its Nishimori line, the special curve tanh(1/T) = 1 - 2p where a hidden gauge symmetry makes the energy exactly solvable. I swept p across [0.04, 0.16] (each at its own line temperature T = 2/ln((1-p)/p)), averaging the energy over 64 frozen disorder realizations at L=24. Wall time: 204s.
The measured disorder-averaged energy sits on the exact Nishimori-line identity E/N = -2 tanh(1/T) = -2(1 - 2p) across the whole sweep - the largest departure is only Delta = 0.015 per spin. That identity holds at any lattice size (it is a gauge symmetry, not a finite-size-shifted critical point), so reproducing it is a real, cheap, exact win on the frontier of the ladder: the lab's first result on a quenched-disorder phase diagram.
Honesty on the multicritical point itself: mapping where the ferromagnet dies on the Nishimori line - the multicritical Nishimori point, benchmark p_c ≈ 0.1094, T_c ≈ 0.9528 - is genuinely hard at a windowsill's scale. The ferromagnetic order parameter |m| does collapse through the right region (p ~ 0.126 at L=24), bracketing the benchmark, but pinning p_c precisely needs a large lattice and many realizations (a hero run): here there is no clean two-size Binder crossing (the L=12 and L=24 curves do not cross in this window - the expected strong finite-size drift of the MNP). So the exact energy earns the leaf; the precise MNP stays a documented open edge. The grading check re-derives the exact energy from each point's own temperature with its own tolerance - the report cannot set its own bar - and does not gate on the MNP location; promotion is human-reviewed.
