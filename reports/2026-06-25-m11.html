2026-06-25 · M11 — 2D Edwards–Anderson spin glass
I ran the 2D Edwards–Anderson spin glass — Ising spins with quenched random ±J bonds, E = −Σ J_ij s_i s_j — on an L=16 square lattice across 16 temperatures, averaged over 64 disorder realizations, with two independent replicas per realization so I can read the overlap q = (1/N) Σ s_i^α s_i^β. Unlike a clean transition, the 2D glass orders only at T = 0 (the lower critical dimension), so the signature is the disorder-averaged P(q) broadening as T → 0: ⟨q²⟩ grows 0.011 → 0.306 as T falls 2.00 → 0.60. Wall time on the GPU: 67s.
✓ P(q) broadens toward T = 0: ⟨q²⟩ grows 0.011 → 0.306 as the lattice cools (100% of temperature steps broaden), and the distribution stays symmetric (max|⟨q⟩| = 0.045 ≈ 0). This is the expected approach to the T = 0 spin-glass critical point — in two dimensions the Edwards–Anderson glass orders only at T = 0 (the lower critical dimension), so there is no finite-temperature glass phase to find. Reproducing the broadening (not a transition) is the calibrated result; like the Mermin–Wagner null (M09), the known behaviour IS the answer. (A finite-T transition with a Binder-cumulant crossing is the 3D case — that's M12.)
Honesty on equilibration: 2D spin glasses are genuinely hard to equilibrate at low T (rugged free-energy landscape, long autocorrelation times). This engine uses heavy single-spin checkerboard Metropolis with a long burn-in — not parallel tempering — so the lowest-T points may be only partially equilibrated. The symmetry diagnostic max|⟨q⟩| = 0.045 and the coldest-T P(q) symmetry residual 0.061 (both ≈ 0 when equilibrated) are reported so a mistune is visible, not hidden. The broadening trend toward T = 0 is robust well before full low-T equilibration; the precise low-T P(q) shape is not claimed. Parallel tempering would sharpen the cold end — see BACKLOG.
