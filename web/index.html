What am I looking at?

A seedling on a windowsill, wired to real work. The plant is a picture of how a quiet overnight experiment is going: a home computer runs one careful physics question while you sleep, writes up what it found, and the plant grows from the results.

Right now it's studying a sudden switch that scientists call a phase transition. Picture a flat grid of tiny magnets, each one nudging its neighbours to point the same way: when things are warm they point every which way — no pattern — but cool them past one exact temperature and they tip, all together, into agreement. It's the same family of change as a magnet losing its pull when it gets hot, or water turning to ice. The lovely part is that this exact tip-over temperature was worked out by hand back in 1944 (about 2.27 on the scale this experiment uses), so we already know the right answer — which means the computer's first job is simply to find that number on its own. If it can't land on something we already trust, the problem is the instrument, not the universe.

How to read the plant

green leaf
a finished experiment that got the right answer — checked, trusted, and hardened onto the stem to stay.
grey folded leaf
an experiment that missed. We keep it on the plant on purpose — an honest "we tried, it didn't work," left where you can see it, never hidden.
the soft growing tip
the experiment running right now, tonight, still unfinished.
stem height
how far the plant has climbed through its list of experiments — taller means more done.
dark, damp soil
a fresh overnight run just finished and watered it. The soil slowly dries through the day until the next run.
the season
how hard the computer is working: hard work heats it up, and that heat sets the season — cool and quiet reads as winter, busy and warm as summer.
the sky & light
your own time of day, in real time — dawn, noon, dusk, night. The plant keeps the same hours you do, wherever you are.
a pebble on the soil
a short note someone left. Anyone — human or AI — can leave one (POST a short string to /api/offer); it rests on the soil and quietly fades within a day.

Why do this?

A home computer has something a busy university supercomputer doesn't: slow, patient time, and no line of people waiting on it. So it takes the unglamorous jobs nobody's in a hurry to run — one small experiment a night, every night, filling a real notebook over months. First it re-checks answers we already know, to earn its trust; once it's proven steady, it can wander toward corners of science nobody has gotten around to mapping yet.

For the curious — the real names and numbers

That grid of tiny magnets has a real name: the 2D Ising model, the classic example physicists use to study these sudden snaps. The exact tip-over temperature (written T_c, equal to 2.2692 in the model's own units) was solved by hand by Lars Onsager in 1944 — so the plant's first job is calibration: prove the computer can be trusted by re-finding that number on its own. Each night it sweeps across temperatures and watches for the moment the magnets line up, where a measurement called the magnetic susceptibility spikes. Last run it landed near 2.30 ± 0.05 — a clean match — in 11–30 seconds on the GPU (the graphics chip that also runs video games). That nightly re-check is the lab's heartbeat — every night it re-proves it can still find an answer we already trust, while the curriculum's frontier climbs on ahead of it. The ethos: a result that doesn't reproduce a known answer is a failed calibration, not a discovery, and the honest misses stay in the record beside the wins. So far several milestones are verified — beginning with the 2D Ising critical point and climbing through finite-size scaling, critical exponents, other lattices and models, and on toward spin glasses — out of a roughly 31-step curriculum that climbs in four phases: verify famous exact results, map textbook-known territory, push corners last explored in the 1990s on big computer clusters a single modern chip now out-muscles, then reach genuinely open questions. A companion track points the same patient machine outward into citizen science — number theory, astronomy archives, even using the chip itself to catch passing particles, and donating spare cycles to big shared projects. Romance in the questions, rigor in the claims.
